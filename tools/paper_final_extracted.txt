 DEVELOPMENT OF A WEB- AND MOBILE-BASED SCHOLARSHIP FINDER AND ELIGIBILITY PLATFORM FOR FILIPINO STUDENTS WITH A DECISION SUPPORT SYSTEM A Capstone Project Presented to the Faculty of Computer Studies Department National College of Science and Technology Dasmariñas, Cavite In Partial Fulfillment of the Requirements for the Degree Bachelor of Science in Information Technology by MERCADO, CHARLES HOWARD ROSARIO, JAMES LOUIS SALDITOS, MANUEL SHAWN ANDRADA, ARISTON E APRIL 2026 Chapter 1 THE PROBLEM AND ITS BACKGROUND Introduction Education is very important for individual development and the growth of the country. A lot of students in the Philippines want to go to college, but they can't because they don't have enough money. Because of this, scholarships from the government, private groups, and other groups have become an important way for students who need money to get it . Even with scholarship programs available, finding information about them is still a challenge. Details about scholarships, including requirements and deadlines, are often spread across different online platforms, social media, school announcements, and other sources. As a result, students must spend time searching different places, comparing eligibility criteria, and determining if they qualify. This process can be confusing and time-consuming, which might cause qualified students to miss out on important opportunities . The platform's objectives encompass the aggregation of scholarship information, the facilitation of opportunity discovery based on student qualifications, and the application of image processing to extract General Weighted Averages (GWA) for streamlined eligibility assessment. Consequently, the proposed platform aims to expedite, simplify, and enhance the scholarship search experience for students . Project Context To assist Filipino students in identifying scholarship opportunities aligned with their academic credentials and location, the researchers created a web- and mobile-based platform. This system streamlines the scholarship search process by automatically extracting a student's General Weighted Average from submitted report cards and subsequently calculating match probabilities via a decision support system. The platform serves students who need financial assistance for their education and administrators who manage scholarship programs, targeting the student community of the National College of Science and Technology and beyond. The Application Review Module is the next module. When a student submits an application, it lands here for review. Administrators can see the student’s details, which scholarship they picked, and the documents they uploaded. With one click, they can say “approved” or “rejected.” If they reject it, they can leave a note explaining why, so the student knows what to fix. The Document Verification Module is the next module. Students need to upload papers like birth certificates and school IDs. This module lets administrators look at those files and mark them as “verified” or “rejected.” If a file is rejected, they can tell the student why. The student sees the status right away, so they know if they need to upload something again. The GWA Extraction Module. Instead of asking students to type their GWA by hand, this module lets them take a picture or upload a PDF of their report card. The system cleans up the image and uses a tool called Tesseract to read the text.It looks for keywords and can also figure out the average from grades.f it sees a percentage, it changes it to the 1.00–5.00 scale used in the Philippines. This saves students time and keeps the GWA correct. The Decision Support System (DSS) Module. After the student’s GWA is saved, this module gets to work. It checks every active scholarship and sees if the student’s GWA meets the requirement. It also looks at the student’s course and compares it to what the scholarship wants. If the student shared their location, it gives a little extra weight to scholarships that are closer. Then it shows each scholarship with a percentage and a simple label like “Ready to Apply” or “Needs Documents.” This helps students quickly spot the scholarships they are most likely to get. The Scholarship Management Module. This module lets providers and administrators create, change, or remove scholarships. They fill out a form with the scholarship name, who is giving it, what it’s about, the GWA requirement, the deadline, and which documents are needed. They can also add a logo, put in a link for an online exam, and even pin multiple exam locations on a map. This keeps the list of scholarships up to date and correct. The Application Wizard Module. Applying for a scholarship can be hard, but this wizard makes it simple. It shows the details and checks if they qualify. It then lists the documents needed, marking progress. Finally, it lets the student check everything, agree to the rules, and submit. The Location & Mapping Module. Students can show where they live by dropping a pin on a map. They can also let the system guess their location from their address, or use their device’s GPS. Scholarship providers can also place pins for their offices or for remote exam sites. When students look at scholarships, they can see how far each one is and even open a map to get directions. This helps them choose opportunities that are close to home. The GWA Issue Reporting Module. Sometimes the system misreads a grade. If that happens, students can report the mistake right from the upload page. They pick a reason, like “The GWA it found is wrong,” and can suggest the correct number. The report is saved so an administrator can check it later. If needed, the admin can manually change the GWA. This makes sure students aren’t hurt by an occasional slip up. The Activity Logging Module. This module quietly writes down everything important that happens—like when someone logs in, submits an application, or uploads a document. Admins can look at this record later. They can see extra details, such as why a document was rejected. The Frontend User Interface Module . Students see this when they visit the website. It looks clean and works well on phones and computers. From the main page, students can look for scholarships, upload their grades, check their documents, and start the application wizard. Buttons and messages are clear, so students always know what to click next. The Email & Notification Service Module. When a student joins, the system emails them a code to check their email is real. If they forget their password, it emails a different code so they can make a new one. Emails are sent through a secure connection, and the system keeps a record of every email sent. This way, users get important messages without anyone having to send them one by one. Statement of the Problem Filipino students have a hard time finding scholarships that fit them. This study asks: 1. How can we put all scholarships in one place that students can easily reach? 2. How can the system automatically show scholarships that match the student’s grades, course, and location? 3. How can students quickly search, compare, and see what each scholarship needs? 4. How can staff easily add, change, or remove scholarships and check student documents? 5. How can the system read a student’s GWA from a report card and let them fix it if it’s wrong? Objectives of the Study This study aims to build a website and app that helps Filipino students find and apply for scholarships. Specifically, it aims to: 1. Put all scholarships in one place so students can easily see them. 2. Show students scholarships that fit their grades, course, and where they live. 3. Tell students what each scholarship requires, and how matched they are. 4. Give staff a simple panel to manage scholarships, users, and check documents. 5. Use OCR to read a student’s GWA from their report card and let them fix it if it’s wrong. Scope and Limitation of the Study This study aims to build a web - and mobile - based tool that helps Filipino college students find scholarships and see if they qualify. The system has a homepage where students can browse scholarships. It also has login and registration pages that ask for the student’s location. There’s a student dashboard, a page for uploading grades that uses image processing to read the GWA, and a results page with a decision support system that shows match percentages and eligibility. Students can upload documents and see what’s done. They can also look at a map of scholarships and use a step-by-step application guide. For staff, there are admin panels where they can manage users, scholarships, applications, and check documents. The decision support system matches students with scholarships by checking simple things: the student’s GWA, their course, how soon the deadline is, and who is giving the scholarship. It also checks which required documents have been uploaded and verified. The image processing part uses standard techniques—grayscale, contrast and brightness adjustments, sharpening—to read the GWA from common report cards and transcripts. It works best when the uploaded file is clear and clean, and it accepts JPG, PNG, and PDF files up to 5MB. The system’s recommendations are only as good as the data entered by staff—scholarship details have to be added manually; the system does not pull information from other websites. It works on both computers and phones as long as there is an internet connection, but it does not work offline and does not send automatic deadline reminders . Significance of the Study Students . Chapter 2 REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE AND STUDIES Local Literature According to the Commission on Higher Education (2025a), the CHED Merit Scholarship Program (CMSP) is a well‑known government scholarship in the Philippines. It gives money to students who get good grades, especially those who meet a certain GWA. The problem is, many students don’t know what that GWA requirement is. The info is spread out—some schools put it on a bulletin board, some hide it in a PDF, and students often have to ask their guidance counselor just to find out. Our system fixes that by keeping all the requirements in one place. When a student uploads their grades, the system automatically checks if their GWA is high enough. Students don’t have to hunt for the rules or try to figure out if they qualify. It just tells them right away. Imagine trying to apply for five different scholarships and having to look up each one’s GWA cutoff—that’s a lot of work and easy to mess up. Our system does it in seconds . According to the Commission on Higher Education (2025b), the Medical Scholarship and Return Service (MSRS) is for students who want to be doctors. It doesn’t just give money for school—after they graduate, they have to serve in places where there aren’t enough doctors. That’s a big commitment, and the scholarship itself is very strict about the course: you have to be in a medical program to even be considered. If you’re a nursing student or a biology major, you don’t qualify. So before the system, a student in medicine might have had to sift through dozens of scholarships just to find the ones meant for them. Our system uses a course‑matching feature to handle this. It looks at what course the student is taking and compares it to what the scholarship requires. If the student is in medicine, that scholarship gets a higher match score and shows up near the top of their list. So they don’t waste time looking at scholarships they can’t qualify for. It’s like having a filter that only shows you what’s actually relevant . According to the Commission on Higher Education (2025c), the Scholarship for Future Statisticians is aimed at students taking statistics or related courses. This shows that not all scholarships are open to everyone—some are very specific. A student in business or engineering might not even know this scholarship exists, because it’s not widely advertised outside statistics programs. In many schools, these kinds of niche scholarships are only mentioned in department announcements, and if you’re not in that department, you might never hear about them. The system helps by letting students search by their course. It also scans the scholarship descriptions for keywords like “statistics” or “data science.” So if a student is in statistics, this scholarship will naturally appear near the top of their results without them having to stumble upon it by accident. It’s like having a personal assistant who knows what you’re studying and points you to the scholarships that fit . According to the Commission on Higher Education (2025d), the Scholarships for Instructors’ Knowledge Advancement Program (SIKAP) is meant for teachers who want to go back to school for further studies. This is a good reminder that scholarships aren’t only for college students—they can also be for instructors, researchers, and other school staff. Often, these opportunities are overlooked because most platforms are built for undergraduates. Our system is built to be flexible. Administrators can add scholarships for any group they choose, whether it’s for undergraduate students, graduate students, teachers, or even researchers. So the platform isn’t limited to one type of user; it can grow to serve different needs. A teacher looking to get a master’s degree can log in and see scholarships specifically for educators, just like a student would see undergraduate scholarships . According to Riño and Daing (2022), keeping student records by hand is a real problem in many schools. They found that papers get lost, files get misplaced, and when a teacher or staff member needs to find a student’s information, it can take forever. Sometimes records are stored in different cabinets or even different offices, making things even harder. Imagine trying to find a specific student’s transcript from three years ago when the filing cabinet is overflowing and there’s no clear system. It’s frustrating and time‑consuming. In our system, we store everything in a database. Students can log in anytime to check their own information, and if staff need to verify something, they can pull it up instantly—no more digging through piles of paper. It just makes the whole process faster and a lot less frustrating. Plus, you never have to worry about a file getting lost or damaged . Foreign Literature According to Anwar, Furqan, and Suhardi (2024), using a decision support system with fuzzy logic can make picking scholarship winners fairer. Fuzzy logic helps computers handle things that aren’t just yes or no—there’s a lot of in‑between. Like when you decide if a student is “good enough” for a scholarship, it’s not just a yes or no; there are different degrees of good. Their research shows computers can use clear rules instead of personal feelings. Our system uses a simpler version. It gives points for good grades, matching courses, far‑off deadlines, and being close to home. Then it adds the points to give a match percentage. It’s not as complicated as fuzzy logic, but it works the same way—let the numbers decide, not feelings. That way, no student gets overlooked just because someone didn’t like their essay; they get a fair shot based on facts . According to Arslantaş , Gümüş, and Özder (2023), methods like AHP and SAW are used to sort students for scholarships. People like these methods because they help make decisions when there’s a lot to think about. When you’re picking who gets a scholarship, you have to look at grades, activities outside school, how much money they need, what course they’re taking—it’s a lot to juggle.What their research shows is that you can’t just look at one thing—you have to consider grades, course, and other factors together. Our system does exactly that. It combines four factors: GWA, course, deadline, and distance. Each factor gets a weight, and together they form a final score. It’s the same idea, just made simpler so it’s easier to understand and implement. For a student, seeing that their GWA got them 40 points, their course matched for 30, etc., makes it clear why they got the score they did . According to Kuswanto et al. (2023), the SAW method is a simple way to give points to help pick scholarship winners. The idea behind SAW is that some things should matter more than others. For example, if grades are the most important, they should have the highest weight. In our system, we follow that same principle. GWA is the most important factor, so it gets 40 points out of 100. Course match comes next with 30 points, then deadline with 15, and distance with 15. That way, the score reflects what really matters for scholarship eligibility, and students can see why they got the score they did. It’s a transparent system—no hidden math, no secrets . According to Martin (2024), using a decision support system saves staff a lot of time because they don’t have to manually check every student. In schools without a system, someone has to sit down and compare each student’s GWA against each scholarship’s requirement. That can take days, especially when there are hundreds of applicants. Imagine a staff member with a stack of papers and a calculator, checking every single student’s grades against a list of scholarships. It’s mind‑numbing work, and mistakes happen. Our system does that work automatically. Administrators don’t have to do the math themselves—the system does it for them. Staff can then focus on checking documents and making final decisions instead of doing repetitive math. It just makes their job easier and lets them focus on what really matters: helping students . According to Novianto (2023), AHP helps you make big, tricky decisions without getting lost. Instead of trying to figure everything out at once, you just compare two things at a time. You might ask yourself, “What’s more important—GWA or matching the course?” Then you compare GWA against the deadline, and so on. It’s basically breaking a big problem into smaller pieces. Our system doesn’t use AHP exactly, but it follows the same basic idea. It looks at a few simple things—like GWA, course, deadline, and how far away the scholarship is—and then mixes them together to give a single result. That way, students can easily see why a scholarship might be a good fit for them, without having to crunch a bunch of numbers themselves.It’s a straightforward way to help them make decisions, like having a checklist that tells you how well you meet each requirement .
